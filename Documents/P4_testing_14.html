Testing Procedure

We did our testing following Tognazzinis 10 testing principles. We introduced ourselves, the task we had been given and how the test would be conducted. We explained they could at any time withdraw from the test if they felt the need. Further we told them the importance of thinking out loud, as this part of the testing precedure is crucial for us to get information from. We also gave them examples of how to think out loud. Then we told them that any difficulties they might have completing the presented tasks is a fault of the website, not them as test subjects. We presented them the option of asking questions before we started the test, and gave them the opportunity to make remarks after the test.

Tested Audience

All test subjects were students, or in the same age-group as students. This is also the websites main target audience. In an optimal testing procedure we would also have liked to test a couple of around 50 year old persons, as the bands parents likely also would want to visit the webpage.

Tested Platforms

We have tested the website on different computers, a mobile phone and a tablet. The different computers have had the operating systems mac OS and Microsoft Windows respectively with the browsers Safari and Chrome. The cellphone we tested on was an iPhone and the tablet was an iPad.

Feedback Summary

The test subjects navigated through the site easily, and we found few errors during the testing. We got feedback from the users that the website had a good flow with the animations and a logical structure, and that it looked aesthetically pleasing in all the different formats. They liked the content of the page, and easily found the information they were looking for. On the other hand we discovered a couple errors during the testing. The first error is the fact that you couldn’t scroll down on the menu from the home page, and the second the fact that you can’t play a youtube video directly on a website from your phone. In addition we got feedback that some of the testers didn’t feel that a plus sign was an intuitive symbol for a menu and that they rather wished that it would just say “menu”, in spite of the fact that all the subjects pressed the plus sign first when they were looking for the menu. All in all we got a lot of positive feedback, and the users had virtually no trouble with the site.

Our Response

After discovering the error with the menu, we fixed it by making sure the user can scroll down and see the whole menu on all devices. When it comes to the youtube-video not playing we could have used a media query that only displayed an image instead of the video when the site was being used on a phone. We could also change the plus-icon to a button that just says menu so it’s easier for the users to understand that it’s a menu. Because of our limited time we have chosen to leave the page as it is now, but if we have had more time we could have implemented these changes.

Self-reflection

When reflecting upon the project and about what we have learned as a team, we found that our team's diverse competence was one of our biggest strengths. As three of us had some background in HTML, CSS, and JS, and one of the team members had a lot of experience in design, we managed to use and build upon each others strengths. Design issues were easily solved by double checking with or asking our "designer", and a quick discussion/question to the computer students often solved coding issues. The kind of "forced" division of the work also helped us by making sure every team member always knew what to do, and the deadline for doing it. Looking back, this both helped us save time in team meetings, and also made sure everyone got to do their "fair share" of work. The fact that our client's contact person also was one of the team members might also have saved us some time, as it was relatively easy to get in contact with him. All in all, this project has not only helped us to learn how to create and design a website, but also given us insight in how to work effectively as a team, structure work, and meeting a client's requirements.
